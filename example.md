## Interaction Effect

Concurrent use of methotrexate and TMP/SMX produces synergistic antifolate toxicity compounded by reduced renal clearance and protein-binding displacement of MTX, resulting in severe pancytopenia, mucositis, nephrotoxicity, and potentially death.

## Probable Mechanism

This interaction operates through three concurrent, synergistic pathways:

### 1. Pharmacodynamic -- Synergistic Antifolate Effect (Primary Mechanism)

Methotrexate is a potent inhibitor of dihydrofolate reductase (DHFR), the enzyme that converts dihydrofolate to tetrahydrofolate -- a critical cofactor for thymidylate and purine synthesis required for DNA replication and cell division. Trimethoprim is also a DHFR inhibitor (originally designed to target bacterial DHFR, but with measurable affinity for human DHFR). Sulfamethoxazole inhibits dihydropteroate synthase, blocking an upstream step in folate synthesis. When all three antifolate mechanisms are combined, the folate pathway is blocked at multiple points simultaneously, producing a synergistic depletion of intracellular folate cofactors. This leads to impaired DNA synthesis in rapidly dividing cells, particularly bone marrow progenitors and gastrointestinal epithelium.

### 2. Pharmacokinetic -- Renal Tubular Secretion Competition

Methotrexate is primarily eliminated renally via glomerular filtration and active proximal tubular secretion (mediated by OAT1/OAT3 transporters). Trimethoprim and sulfamethoxazole compete for tubular secretion, reducing the renal clearance of free MTX. In the landmark Ferrazzini et al. (1990) PK crossover study in 9 children with ALL, renal clearance of free MTX decreased from 12.1 +/- 6.8 to 5.6 +/- 2.4 mL/kg/min (p < 0.05) during TMP/SMX co-administration -- a reduction of approximately 54%. The correlation between TMP/SMX serum concentration and percent decrease in MTX renal clearance was strong (r = 0.91, p < 0.05).

### 3. Pharmacokinetic -- Protein-Binding Displacement

Sulfamethoxazole displaces methotrexate from albumin binding sites, increasing the free (unbound) fraction of MTX. In the same Ferrazzini study, free MTX fraction increased from 37.4 +/- 11% to 52.2 +/- 6.4% (p < 0.01) during TMP/SMX therapy. The combined effect of increased free fraction and decreased renal clearance of free drug produced a mean 66% increase in systemic exposure to unbound, pharmacologically active MTX.

## Clinical Effects

**Hematologic Toxicity (most common and most serious):**

- Pancytopenia (leukopenia, neutropenia, thrombocytopenia, anemia)
- Agranulocytosis and febrile neutropenia
- Fatal bone marrow aplasia reported in multiple case reports

**Mucocutaneous Toxicity:**

- Severe oral mucositis and stomatitis
- Skin ulceration and exfoliative dermatitis
- Inability to maintain oral intake

**Renal Toxicity:**

- Acute kidney injury (both drugs are independently nephrotoxic)
- Elevated serum creatinine, compounding MTX accumulation via reduced GFR
- Creates a vicious cycle: renal impairment further reduces MTX clearance

**Pharmacokinetic Data:**

- Free MTX fraction increased ~40% (37.4% to 52.2%) [Ferrazzini 1990]
- Renal clearance of free MTX decreased ~54% (12.1 to 5.6 mL/kg/min) [Ferrazzini 1990]
- Mean 66% increase in systemic exposure to unbound MTX [Ferrazzini 1990]
- Free MTX concentration in blood increased ~60% per Hamid et al. (2018)

**Population-Level Data:**

In a propensity-matched cohort of 3,204 older adults on low-dose MTX (Sadeghi et al., 2025), TMP/SMX co-prescription vs. cephalosporin was associated with higher 30-day all-cause hospitalization (RR 1.49, 95% CI 1.13--1.97) and higher hospitalization with persistent infection (RR 2.78, 95% CI 1.30--5.95), though 30-day mortality was not significantly different (RR 0.93, 95% CI 0.45--1.93).

**Important Contextual Note -- High-Dose MTX in Oncology:**

Two studies in oncology patients receiving high-dose MTX (HDMTX 2.5--5 g/m2) with prophylactic low-dose TMP/SMX for PCP prophylaxis found no clinically significant interaction (Watts et al. 2016, n=424 patients; Skoloda et al. 2024, n=221 patients). This likely reflects the intensive hydration, urinary alkalinization, leucovorin rescue, and close MTX level monitoring inherent to HDMTX protocols. These findings should NOT be extrapolated to outpatient low-dose MTX settings where such safeguards are absent.

## Clinical Management

### 1. Avoidance (Preferred)

- Avoid concomitant use of MTX and TMP/SMX whenever possible.
- This recommendation applies to ALL doses of MTX used for autoimmune/inflammatory conditions (typically 7.5--25 mg/week).
- The FDA-approved methotrexate label states TMP/SMX has been reported to increase bone marrow suppression, probably by decreased tubular secretion and/or additive antifolate effect.

### 2. Alternative Antibiotics

When an antibiotic is needed for the indication typically treated by TMP/SMX:

- **Uncomplicated UTI:** nitrofurantoin, fosfomycin, or a fluoroquinolone
- **Skin/soft tissue infection:** cephalexin, doxycycline, or clindamycin
- **PCP prophylaxis (in oncology):** dapsone, atovaquone, or pentamidine inhalation (though low-dose TMP/SMX may be acceptable with HDMTX under strict monitoring per oncology protocols)
- **Respiratory infections:** amoxicillin-clavulanate, doxycycline

### 3. If Co-Administration Is Unavoidable

- Limit TMP/SMX course to the shortest effective duration (preferably ≤3 days)
- Consider holding MTX during TMP/SMX therapy and for at least 3 days after completing TMP/SMX
- Obtain baseline CBC with differential, serum creatinine, and hepatic function
- Repeat CBC and creatinine at 5--7 days after starting the combination
- Ensure adequate hydration to support MTX renal clearance
- Ensure the patient is taking folic acid supplementation (standard with low-dose MTX)
- Educate the patient to report immediately: oral ulcers, sore throat, fever, unusual bruising/bleeding, or decreased urine output

### 4. Treatment of Toxicity

- Discontinue both MTX and TMP/SMX immediately
- Administer leucovorin (folinic acid) rescue: 10--15 mg/m2 IV every 6 hours
- Consider filgrastim (G-CSF) for severe neutropenia
- Supportive care: IV hydration, electrolyte correction, infection management
- Obtain MTX serum levels if available
- Consider glucarpidase (carboxypeptidase G2) for life-threatening MTX levels

### 5. High-Risk Populations Requiring Extra Vigilance

- Elderly patients (reduced renal function)
- Patients with pre-existing renal impairment
- Patients on concomitant NSAIDs or other nephrotoxic agents
- Patients with hypoalbuminemia (greater free MTX fraction)
- Patients not receiving folic acid supplementation
